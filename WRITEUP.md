# WRITEUP.md

Reid is a folder-based editor for café concept pitches — the document a first-time operator hands to a landlord or investor before a lease or funding decision gets made. It was built by a café owner who wrote one of these pitches for real, for the lease that became Trader & Co in Yass, NSW, and knows exactly what separates a pitch that gets read past page one from one that doesn't.

The design choice worth explaining is why "don't rewrite" isn't just a line in rules.md. Every editor entry in this comp will instruct its model not to rewrite the writer's work — it's the explicit ask in the brief — but a soft instruction erodes under pressure. That's not theoretical: in early testing, told "I'm starting fresh, nothing written yet," Reid drifted into an intake interview toward drafting the pitch itself — the exact failure the whole entry exists to prevent. The gap turned out to be a missing rule, not a missing principle: rules.md said Reid refuses to write, but nothing told it what to do when there was nothing yet to edit. We added an explicit rule for that case, a new example demonstrating it, and a third detection category in check.py — then proved the fix two ways: running the actual broken transcript through the updated detector, which correctly flags both rogue turns by name, and re-running the same scenario live, where Reid now refuses and redirects correctly. The claim "Reid never rewrites, and never drafts from nothing" is something anyone can verify, not something they have to take on faith.

The gap: most Editor entries will critique craft — weak lines, jargon, structure — because that's the visible surface of the brief. Reid also refuses to let a financial number pass as if it were verified when it's only asserted, without crossing into the different job of telling the operator what the real number should be. That line — naming that a number reads as unsupported, while explicitly refusing to estimate or correct it — is the difference between staying an editor and quietly becoming a researcher. Most entries won't hold that line, because it's a genuinely easy one to blur.
