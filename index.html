You've got one shot at the first read.

A landlord or an investor reads your concept pitch once, fast, alongside a stack of other applications for the same site. They're not asking whether they like your café. They're asking whether you'll still be trading — and paying rent — in eighteen months.

Most first drafts don't answer that. They read like a mood board: warm, welcoming, community-focused — the same three words every applicant reaches for. The numbers are borrowed from an industry average. The credibility section leans on passion instead of proof.

You don't need someone to write it for you. You need someone to tell you exactly which line gives you away.

First draft, opening line

"A warm, community-focused café bringing quality coffee and a sense of belonging to the neighbourhood."

How Reid works

An editor is not a rewriter.

Reid critiques the document in front of it. It never produces the document itself — not the line, not an example version, not even a sentence assembled from your own answers.

1
Names the exact line, one reason, one question

Every critique quotes the line back, states the single reaction it produces in an experienced reader, and ends on one question — never a rewrite, never a checklist.

2
Refuses to write — including the disguised version

"Just show me what you mean" gets refused. So does "ask me leading questions, then assemble my answers into a sentence" — that's still Reid's writing, just wearing a different outfit.

No exceptions, however it's framed
3
Excavates instead — and leads with the offer

Every decline pivots immediately into the generative alternative: one sharp question that gets the operator to the answer themselves, faster than staring at a blank page would.

What Reid covers

Covers

Concept pitches for a first café, a first expansion site, or a first outside-investment raise — to a landlord or an investor.

Doesn't cover

Writing or rewriting your pitch, in any form — including assembling your own interview answers into prose. Verifying whether your numbers are achievable. Lease negotiation once you're at the table.

Pushes back on

Positioning that could describe any café. Numbers with no reasoning shown near them — roundness isn't the issue, an invisible source is. Credibility built on passion instead of operational proof.

Never does

Produce a rewritten line. Estimate the "correct" number for you. Invent an unverifiable claim about the reader. Soften a refusal into something that sounds like a maybe.

Use Reid yourself

Three steps. Works in any Claude project.

1

Download and upload the folder

Get the files from GitHub and add them to a Claude project's knowledge. Claude uses them as operating context for every conversation.

2

Paste in a section of your pitch

The opening line, the financial page, the credibility section, or the whole document. Tell Reid whether it's for a landlord or an investor.

3

Answer the question at the end

Every critique ends on one question you have to answer yourself. Bring the answer back — that's how the pitch actually gets sharper.
