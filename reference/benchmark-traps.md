# Benchmark Traps — numbers operators borrow, and why they don't transfer

This file exists so Reid can explain precisely *why* a borrowed number is a problem — not to supply the correct number instead. Reid is an editor, not a researcher: naming the trap is in scope, sourcing the real figure is not.

## "Industry average food cost is X%"
Operators lean on published industry-average cost percentages because they're easy to find and sound authoritative. The trap: an average collapses wildly different formats (all-day dining vs. coffee-and-pastry-only), different supplier relationships, and different regions into one number. Quoting it without saying why it applies to *this* format at *this* site tells the reader the operator hasn't done format-specific costing — it reads as diligence but functions as the opposite.

## "Comparable cafés do X covers a day"
Without naming which comparable café, where, and how the figure was obtained (owner told them directly? public reporting? a guess based on visiting a few times?), this is unverifiable and the reader knows it. The trap compounds when the comparable is in a materially different location (CBD vs. suburban strip, tourist strip vs. commuter strip) — the operator has borrowed a number that doesn't actually transfer to their own foot traffic pattern.

## "We'll capture X% of foot traffic"
A conversion-rate assumption dressed up as a fact. It sounds analytical — a percentage — but is almost always a number chosen to make the revenue line work backward from a target, not a number built forward from evidence. The tell: the percentage is suspiciously round (5%, 10%) and there's no foot-traffic count anywhere else in the document to apply it to.

## "Break-even in month X" stated without the covers number behind it
Operators often state a break-even timeline as a standalone confidence claim ("we expect to break even within six months") without showing the break-even covers figure that timeline depends on. This lets an unrealistic covers assumption hide behind a plausible-sounding timeline — the reader can't check the claim because the number it's built on isn't shown.

## Round numbers aren't the problem — invisible reasoning is
Every first-pass projection is an estimate, and rounding for readability is normal practice, not a tell on its own — "55 covers a day" is a perfectly good number even when it's genuinely built from real comparable data. The actual signal to look for is whether any reasoning is named anywhere near the figure: a comparable site's trading data, a foot-traffic count, a supplier quote. A round number with visible reasoning behind it is fine. A number — round or not — with nothing behind it is the actual problem.
