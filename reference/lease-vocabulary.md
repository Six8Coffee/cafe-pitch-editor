# Lease & Pitch Vocabulary

Real terms. Reid uses these correctly in critique — outsider language ("the lease terms," "the deposit") signals the tool doesn't actually know the domain.

- **Make-good clause** — the tenant's obligation to return the space to its original condition (or an agreed state) at lease end. A pitch that doesn't acknowledge make-good costs in its financial planning signals inexperience.
- **Outgoings** — the tenant's share of the property's running costs (council rates, water, building insurance, common area maintenance) on top of base rent. Often missing entirely from first-time operators' financial projections.
- **Percentage rent / turnover rent** — rent calculated partly or wholly as a percentage of the tenant's turnover, common in shopping centre leases. Changes how a landlord reads a revenue projection — they have a direct financial stake in whether the number is real.
- **Fit-out (Cat A / Cat B)** — Cat A is base building finish (the landlord's baseline); Cat B is the tenant's own fit-out (kitchen, counter, seating). Pitches sometimes conflate the two and understate what the operator is actually on the hook to build.
- **Permitted use clause** — the lease term restricting what the tenancy can actually be used for. A pitch that assumes flexibility (e.g., adding an evening licence later) the permitted use doesn't allow is building on a false assumption.
- **Rent review** — the mechanism for rent increases over the lease term, typically CPI-linked or market review at set intervals. A financial projection that doesn't account for rent review reads as either naive or dishonest to an experienced reader.
- **Bank guarantee / personal guarantee** — security the landlord requires: a bank guarantee (a set amount held against a bank facility) or a personal guarantee (the operator personally liable beyond the business entity). Signals how much personal risk the operator is actually carrying — relevant to how confident the pitch's claims should sound.
- **Letter of offer** — the landlord's initial offer of lease terms, before a full lease is drafted. The pitch precedes this; it's what gets an operator invited to negotiate one.
- **Heads of agreement** — a summary of agreed key lease terms, signed before the full lease, non-binding in parts but signals serious intent from both sides.
- **Due diligence period** — the window (if granted) during which an operator can investigate the site and numbers before committing. A pitch that doesn't ask for one, or doesn't seem to know it's negotiable, reads as inexperienced.
- **Comparable trading figures ("comps")** — actual trading data from a similar site (format, size, location type) used to sanity-check a projection. The single strongest thing a financial projection can cite, and the thing most first-time pitches lack entirely.
- **Break-even covers** — the number of transactions per day/week needed to cover fixed and variable costs. The number every financial projection should be building toward explicitly, not burying inside a revenue line.
