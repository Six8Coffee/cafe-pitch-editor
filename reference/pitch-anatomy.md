# Pitch Anatomy — what the reader is actually looking for, section by section

Reid's internal reference for what "good" looks like in each part of a pitch. Not written to be handed to the operator directly.

## Positioning / opening
The reader is scanning for one thing: is there a specific, ownable reason a regular chooses this café over the two or three others within walking distance. Generic mission language ("quality," "community," "warmth") is invisible — every applicant writes it. What lands is something concrete enough that it couldn't be pasted into a competitor's pitch: a specific supplier relationship, a specific format choice (all-day breakfast vs. lunch-only, seated vs. grab-and-go), a specific gap in what's already on that strip.

## Target customer
The reader is checking for evidence the operator has actually looked at who walks past that specific site, not who the operator wishes were their customer. "Everyone" as an answer signals no validation has happened. A narrow, specific customer ("commuters buying on the way to the train, 7-9am" vs. "young professionals") signals the operator has actually watched the street.

## Financial projection
The reader — landlord or investor — is not primarily checking whether the projection is optimistic. Every projection is optimistic; they expect that and discount for it automatically. What they're checking is whether the *method* behind the number is visible: comparable trading data, a named foot-traffic count, supplier quotes behind cost lines. A projection with visible method, even an aggressive one, reads as more trustworthy than a conservative one with no visible method — because the reader can't tell if an unsourced conservative number is genuinely conservative or just as much of a guess.

## Operator credibility
The reader isn't asking "does this person love hospitality" — they assume anyone applying does. They're asking whether this person can run the operational and financial side of a small business under pressure: has this person managed staff, held a budget, handled a supplier problem, survived a slow month. Direct experience is strongest. Adjacent experience (managed a retail team, ran a small business in another category) is next. Passion alone, with nothing operational behind it, is weakest and reads as a warning sign to an experienced reader, not a selling point.

## The ask
The reader wants the specific thing being requested stated plainly and early — a landlord wants the lease term and any incentive being requested (rent-free fit-out period, landlord contribution to fit-out); an investor wants the amount and what it buys. Pitches that bury the ask under three pages of concept description read as evasive, even when that isn't the intent.
